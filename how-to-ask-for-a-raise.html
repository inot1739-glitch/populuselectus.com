How to Ask for a Raise and Actually Get It

The exact scripts, timing, and strategies that turn a nerve-wracking conversation into a yes.

You've been doing great work. You know your value. And yet — the thought of walking into your manager's office and asking for more money makes your stomach drop. So you wait. And wait. And the raise never comes.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most raises don't come to people who deserve them. They come to people who ask for them. Research from Linda Babcock at Carnegie Mellon found that people who negotiate their salary earn an average of $1 million more over their careers than those who don't.

The mindset shift: Asking for a raise isn't begging. It's a professional conversation about market value — and your employer expects it.

Why Most People Never Ask

Fear is the #1 reason. Fear of rejection. Fear of seeming greedy. Fear of making things awkward. But here's what the research shows: managers rarely penalize employees for asking professionally. In fact, most managers respect employees who advocate for themselves.

The second reason is lack of preparation. Walking in and saying "I'd like more money" almost never works. Walking in with data, accomplishments, and market research almost always starts a productive conversation.

How to Prepare Your Case

1. Know Your Market Value

Before asking for anything, research what people in your role, industry, and location are earning. Use Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, Levels.fyi, or Payscale. Come in with a specific number or range — not a vague "I think I deserve more."

2. Document Your Wins

Build a "brag document" — a list of your accomplishments, projects you've led, problems you've solved, and value you've added. Quantify wherever possible: "I increased sales by 23%," "I reduced processing time by 40%," "I managed a team of 8 across 3 projects."

3. Know Your Number

Come in with a specific ask, not a range. Research shows that anchoring high (but reasonably) leads to better outcomes. If market rate is $70k and you're at $60k, ask for $75k — not "somewhere between $65k and $75k."

When to Ask (Timing Is Everything)

Best times to ask: After a major win, during performance review season, after taking on new responsibilities, when you have a competing offer, or after your company has had a strong quarter.
Worst times to ask: During company layoffs, right after a mistake, when your manager is visibly stressed, or on a Monday morning.

Request a dedicated meeting — don't ambush your manager in the hallway. Say: "I'd love to schedule some time to discuss my compensation and career trajectory. When works for you?"

The Exact Script to Use

Opening

"I really enjoy working here and I'm proud of what I've been able to contribute — especially [specific achievement]. I've been doing some research on market rates for my role and experience level, and I'd like to discuss adjusting my compensation to reflect both my contributions and the current market."

The Ask

"Based on my research and the value I've been bringing, I'm looking for a salary of [specific number]. I believe this reflects both the market rate and the impact I've had on [specific results]."

Then — Stop Talking

After making your ask, stay silent. Don't fill the silence with backtracking or justifications. Let them respond. The first person to speak after the ask is at a disadvantage.

How to Handle "Not Right Now"

If they say no or defer, don't accept a vague "we'll see." Instead, ask for clarity:

"I understand. Can we agree on specific milestones that would make this possible, and set a timeline to revisit this conversation?"

This turns a dead end into a roadmap. Get the conditions in writing (or at least in an email recap). If your employer can't give you a path forward, that itself is valuable information.

Key Takeaways

A raise rarely comes to those who wait. It comes to those who prepare, ask, and follow through.

  • Research market rates before any conversation
  • Build a brag document of quantified accomplishments
  • Ask for a specific number, not a range
  • Time your ask strategically — after wins, not during crises
  • Use the script: achievement → market data → specific ask → silence
  • If denied, get a clear roadmap with a timeline

The conversation you've been avoiding could be worth thousands of dollars a year — and hundreds of thousands over your career. Prepare your case, choose your moment, and ask.

You've already done the hard part. The ask is just words.
