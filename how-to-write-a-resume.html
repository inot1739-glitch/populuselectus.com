How to Write a Resume That Gets You Hired in 2026

Most resumes get rejected in 7 seconds. Here's how to be in the 2% that don't.

You spent hours on your resume. You submitted it to 30 jobs. You heard nothing back. Sound familiar?

The problem almost certainly isn't your experience — it's your resume format, your language, and whether you're passing the filters that stand between your application and a human being. In 2026, getting hired starts with understanding how the system actually works.

The Reality of Modern Hiring

Most large companies use Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) — software that scans and filters resumes before a human ever sees them. If your resume doesn't pass the ATS, it doesn't matter how qualified you are. You need to win twice: first against the algorithm, then against a recruiter with 7 seconds of attention.

Beat the ATS Filter First

Use a simple, clean format. No tables, no text boxes, no graphics, no columns. ATS software often can't read these and will garble your resume. Use a single-column layout with standard section headers.
Mirror the job description's language. If the job posting says "project management," your resume should say "project management" — not "managing projects." ATS matches exact keywords. Copy the exact terminology from the job posting.
Use standard file formats. Always submit as PDF unless the job posting specifically requests Word (.docx). Name your file: FirstName-LastName-Resume.pdf.
Include the job title you're applying for. Put it at the top of your resume, right below your name. ATS systems frequently scan for this match.

The 6 Sections Every Resume Needs

1. Header. Full name (large, visible), professional email, phone number, LinkedIn URL, city and country. No photo, no date of birth, no full address.
2. Professional Summary (3 sentences max). Who you are, what you're best at, and what you're looking for. Tailor this to every job. Example: "Results-driven marketing manager with 5 years of experience in B2B SaaS. Specialized in content strategy and SEO. Seeking to drive growth at a mission-driven tech company."
3. Work Experience (reverse chronological). For each role: company name, your title, dates, location — then 3-4 bullet points. The formula: Action verb + what you did + measurable result. "Increased email open rates by 34% through A/B testing subject lines."
4. Skills. Hard skills relevant to the role. Match the terminology in job postings. Don't list soft skills like "teamwork" — they add nothing.
5. Education. Degree, institution, graduation year. That's it unless you're a recent graduate — then you can add relevant coursework or GPA if above 3.5.
6. Optional: Certifications, Projects, or Volunteering. Only if directly relevant to the role. Quality over quantity.

5 Mistakes That Get You Rejected

1. Responsibilities instead of achievements. "Managed social media accounts" is a job description. "Grew Instagram following from 2,000 to 18,000 in 8 months" is an achievement. Recruiters want results, not duties.
2. One resume for all jobs. Every application needs a tailored resume. At minimum, customize your professional summary and match the keywords from the job posting. A growth mindset means treating each application as a learning opportunity — not a copy-paste exercise.
3. Too long. 0-5 years experience: 1 page. 5-10 years: 1-2 pages. 10+ years: 2 pages maximum. Recruiters don't read page 3.
4. Unprofessional email address. coolboy93@hotmail.com gets you rejected immediately. Use firstname.lastname@gmail.com.
5. No quantified results. Every bullet point should answer: "So what?" If you can't add a number, add a scale: "Led a team of 8," "across 3 markets," "for a $2M product line."
Key Takeaways

A great resume wins twice — against the ATS filter and against a recruiter's 7-second scan. Both require different tactics.

  • Use a simple single-column format — no graphics or tables
  • Mirror exact keywords from the job posting for ATS
  • Lead with a 3-sentence tailored professional summary
  • Write achievements, not responsibilities — always include numbers
  • One page for under 5 years experience, max 2 pages for anyone
  • Tailor every resume to the specific job — never send the same one twice

Your resume is a marketing document — and you're the product. Write it like one. Lead with your strongest results, speak the language of the job, and make it effortless for a tired recruiter to see why you're worth 30 more seconds of their time.
