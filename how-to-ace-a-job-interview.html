How to Ace a Job Interview: 10 Tips That Work in 2026

Most candidates prepare the wrong things. Here's what actually gets you the offer.

Most interview advice is generic: "dress professionally," "make eye contact," "be yourself." It's not wrong — it's just not enough. In 2026, with AI-assisted screening and increasingly competitive job markets, the candidates who get offers are the ones who treat the interview as a skill — and prepare accordingly.

Before the Interview: 3 Things Most People Skip

1. Research beyond the website. Everyone reads the "About Us" page. Go further: read recent news about the company, check their LinkedIn for recent hires and departures, find the interviewer on LinkedIn and note their background. Knowing that your interviewer spent 8 years at a competitor gives you context for how to frame your experience.
2. Prepare your STAR stories. STAR = Situation, Task, Action, Result. Prepare 5–7 specific stories from your experience that demonstrate key competencies: leadership, problem-solving, conflict resolution, initiative, failure and recovery. Most behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time when...") can be answered with one of these stories.
3. Prepare your questions. "Do you have any questions for us?" is not a formality — it's an evaluation. Weak candidates say "I think you've covered everything." Strong candidates ask: "What does success look like in this role in the first 90 days?" or "What's the biggest challenge the team is facing right now?"

During the Interview: 7 Techniques That Work

4. Control your first 30 seconds. Research shows interviewers form strong first impressions within 30 seconds. Firm handshake (or confident greeting on video), genuine smile, direct eye contact, and a calm opening. Practice your "tell me about yourself" answer until it's fluid — it's the most predictable question in every interview.
5. Answer questions with structure. Use STAR for behavioral questions. For situational questions, think out loud: "Let me think through this..." gives you processing time and shows analytical ability. Silence before answering is fine — it signals thoughtfulness, not confusion.
6. Quantify everything. "I improved sales" is weak. "I increased sales by 34% over 6 months by restructuring the outreach sequence" is compelling. Numbers create credibility and specificity. If you don't have exact numbers, use ranges: "roughly 30–40%."
7. Show genuine curiosity about the role. Interviewers hire people who want THIS job, not A job. Reference specific things you learned in your research: "I noticed the company recently expanded into the EU market — how does this role contribute to that strategy?"
8. Handle weaknesses correctly. Don't say "I'm a perfectionist" — it's a cliché that signals inauthenticity. Pick a real weakness that doesn't disqualify you for the role, describe what you've done to address it, and show progress. This demonstrates self-awareness and a growth mindset — both highly valued by employers.
9. Recover from bad answers. If you stumble, it's fine to say "Let me rephrase that" or "Actually, a better example would be..." Interviewers don't expect perfection. They're evaluating how you handle pressure — which is exactly what they need to know for the job.
10. Negotiate salary — always. When asked about salary expectations, don't give a number first. Say "I'd love to understand the full compensation package before discussing numbers." When you do negotiate, anchor high with research: use Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, and industry reports to know your market value before the conversation.

After the Interview: What 90% of Candidates Forget

Send a thank-you email within 24 hours. Short, specific, and professional. Reference something from the conversation. This isn't just politeness — it's a second touch point that keeps you top of mind and demonstrates follow-through. Most candidates don't do it. That's your advantage.

The same discipline that gets you through a job interview — preparation, consistency, follow-through — is what builds long-term career success. Once you have the role, knowing how to negotiate a raise is the next skill to develop.

Key Takeaways

Interviews are a skill — and like any skill, preparation and practice determine the outcome more than talent.

  • Research beyond the company website — read news, find the interviewer on LinkedIn
  • Prepare 5–7 STAR stories covering key competencies
  • Prepare 3 strong questions to ask — "I have no questions" costs you the offer
  • Quantify every achievement — numbers create credibility
  • Handle weaknesses with authenticity and evidence of growth
  • Negotiate salary — always anchor high with market research
  • Send a thank-you email within 24 hours — most candidates don't
